\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The disaggregated Keynesian cross of \citet{Teglio2025} shows how
heterogeneity in the marginal propensity to consume opens an output gap: when
income accrues to agents who do not spend it, demand leaks out of the
circular flow and the economy settles below capacity. The mechanism is a
demand mechanism, and the supply side plays no active role: capacity is given.

This paper asks what happens when the supply side is made endogenous in the
most direct way available --- by letting firms invest out of their own
profits and accumulate capital. The extension is not merely additive. Once
capital accumulates \emph{and} labour is hired endogenously, two channels
that point in opposite directions become active simultaneously:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Supply.} Higher retention $\rho$ means more investment, more
  capital, and higher productive capacity. Taken alone, this raises output.
  \item \textbf{Demand.} In a demand-constrained regime, more capital means
  \emph{fewer workers are needed to serve the same demand}. Employment falls,
  the wage bill falls, and because workers have a higher propensity to consume
  than capitalists, demand falls with it.
\end{itemize}

Which channel dominates is the classical wage-led versus profit-led question
\citep{BhaduriMarglin1990,LavoieStockhammer2013}, and this paper's first
result is that its answer is not a property of the model but a function of a
single technological parameter: the elasticity of substitution between
capital and labour, $\sigma$.

\paragraph{Three contributions.}
First, we build and calibrate a stock-flow-consistent agent-based model in
which the wage-led/profit-led sign is an \emph{outcome} rather than an
assumption, and we locate the frontier $\sigstar$ at which it turns
(\cref{sec:frontier}). Second, we subject that frontier to four independent
stress tests --- wage flexibility, adaptive expectations, fiscal policy, and
firm heterogeneity --- and report that three explicit stabilisation
hypotheses are falsified, all three through the same
wage$\,\to\,U\,\to\,$capital-erosion channel (\cref{sec:stress},
\cref{sec:discussion}). Third, we run a global sensitivity analysis over the
full defensible parameter space and find that the headline result does not
survive marginalisation, that viability is governed by the depreciation rate
rather than by $\sigma$, and that at the empirically implied depreciation
rate the model does not exist at all (\cref{sec:sa}).

\paragraph{A note on what this paper reports.}
An unusual proportion of what follows is negative. Three hypotheses are
falsified; one headline is shown not to generalise; one contradiction is left
explicitly open. This is deliberate. The project operates under a declared
rule --- restated in \cref{sec:method} --- that every number entering a table
or a figure must either be measured from a committed, reproducible driver or
be labelled a design target. The rule was adopted after an audit found that
several previously recorded ``results'' were in fact design targets that had
never been measured. Its consequence is that results which contradict the
project's own expectations are reported rather than recalibrated away, and
the reader should read the negative findings as the paper's substance, not
as its residue.

The remainder is organised as follows. \Cref{sec:literature} places the model
in the literature. \Cref{sec:model} presents it. \Cref{sec:calibration}
documents parameter anchoring, distinguishing estimates from conventions.
\Cref{sec:method} states the experimental protocol. \Cref{sec:baseline} to
\cref{sec:sa} report results. \Cref{sec:discussion} draws the two
cross-cutting readings, \cref{sec:limits} states the limitations, and
\cref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
