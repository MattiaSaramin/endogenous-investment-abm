\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We extend the disaggregated Keynesian cross of \citet{Teglio2025} with
endogenous investment and capital accumulation, so that both the demand and
the supply side of a heterogeneous-agent economy are endogenous. Production
is a \emph{normalised} CES \citep{KlumpDeLaGrandville2000} with elasticity of
substitution~$\sigma$; firms hire endogenously subject to three limits, the
wage follows a Blanchflower--Oswald wage curve, investment is financed
internally out of retained earnings, and the model is stock-flow consistent
across the whole parameter space. The central question is Kaleckian: does
higher retention raise output by building capacity (supply), or lower it by
displacing workers and shrinking the wage bill (demand)?

We find that the answer is governed by~$\sigma$, and we locate a \emph{sign
frontier} $\sigstar \approx 0.65$ at which $\mathrm{d}Y/\mathrm{d}\rho$
changes sign. Three separate stabilisation hypotheses --- slower demand
expectations, a balanced-budget unemployment benefit, and that same benefit
as a cushion against firm heterogeneity --- are each \emph{falsified} by the
data they were designed to test, and all three fail through the same channel:
a wage$\,\to\,$unemployment$\,\to\,$capital-erosion feedback that originates
in the wage curve and is immune to demand-side instruments. This channel,
rather than the sign frontier, is the model's most robust finding.

A global sensitivity analysis over sixteen parameters then shows that the
headline does \emph{not} survive marginalisation: wage-led outcomes are the
exception ($P \approx 0.095$ conditional on viability), only $48.3\%$ of the
empirically defensible space is viable at all, and viability is governed
almost entirely by the depreciation rate $\delta$ ($S_T \approx 1.00$) rather
than by~$\sigma$ ($S_T = 0.024$). At the depreciation rate the data imply for
this model's perimeter ($\delta \approx 0.09$), \emph{zero} of 843 sampled
points survive --- a structural limitation of a capital block without trend
growth, not a parameter to be tuned. We report the resulting tension between
the conditional and the marginal reading of $\sigstar$ as \emph{open}, and
describe the bridging experiment that separates its two candidate causes,
together with its first, partial results.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} agent-based macroeconomics; stock-flow
consistency; elasticity of substitution; wage-led growth; global sensitivity
analysis; capital accumulation.

\end{abstract}
