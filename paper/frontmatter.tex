\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We extend \citet{Teglio2025}'s disaggregated Keynesian cross with endogenous
investment and capital accumulation. Production is a \emph{normalised} CES
\citep{KlumpDeLaGrandville2000} with elasticity~$\sigma$; firms hire
endogenously, the wage follows a
Blanchflower--Oswald curve, and the model is stock-flow consistent throughout.
Whether higher retention $\rho$ builds capacity or displaces workers is an
outcome, not an assumption.

Three results. First, $Y(\rho)$ is \emph{U-shaped}: its turning point $\rhostar$
rises with $\sigma$, and the anchored retention rate lies \emph{left} of that
turn at every elasticity tested, so the marginal effect there is negative, the
wide-range effect positive. Through a single slope this is a sign frontier
$\sigstar \approx 0.65$ --- a declared reduction, not a verdict. Second, the
region where retention depresses output is conditional on fiscal institutions:
a balanced-budget benefit at an OECD-band replacement rate removes it. Third,
the wage curve generates a fragility immune to demand instruments: three
stabilisation hypotheses fail through one
wage$\,\to\,$unemployment$\,\to\,$capital-erosion channel.

A sixteen-parameter global sensitivity analysis separates what generalises from
what does not. \textbf{The negative margin at the anchored $\rho$ survives
marginalisation, holding at $89\%$ of design points where the turn is
resolved} --- though it resolves a turn at only a third of viable points, and
elsewhere we decline to extrapolate one. The \emph{frontier} does not
generalise: $\sigma$ is nearly inert once the other parameters move, so a sign
crossing inside the tested range is conditional, not structural. Viability is
governed almost entirely by the depreciation rate, at whose empirically implied
value no sampled configuration survives. And the measured sign depends on the
estimator chosen to summarise the curve.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} agent-based macroeconomics; stock-flow
consistency; elasticity of substitution; wage-led growth; global sensitivity
analysis; capital accumulation.

\end{abstract}
